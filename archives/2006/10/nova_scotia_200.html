October 05, 2006

Nova Scotia 2006 - the southwest


South of Digby is Bear River.  We had lunch in the cafe in the gold building in the picture.  It was excellent and later when we asked in Digby for a good restaurant we were advised to go to Bear River as it was the best food in the area.  Sometimes you get lucky.


Digby is the entrance to the Digby Neck and Islands, a long strip of land that runs into the Bay of Fundy parallel to the mainland.  We travelled the length of it, even taking two ferries to reach the end.  


One of the most interesting rock formations we have seen recently (and we have seen a lot) was at Balancing Rock on Long Island (the first of the islands on the Neck). A huge slab of rock – probably 18’ high and 2’ in diameter sits on one edge only.  There is a story that local fishermen put ropes around it and tried to pull it down – to no avail.  No one we talked to could confirm or deny the story!



 Along the coast road between Digby & Yarmouth is Church Point where we toured the 1905 Roman Catholic Church.  It is the largest wooden churches in North America.  Most of the columns in the interior were painted to look like marble and it is very difficult to tell otherwise.


The spire rises 56 metres (185 feet).  There are many beautiful original stained glass windows that were brought from France.  Since they didn’t have packing peanuts back then, they shipped each window in large casks or crates of molasses.  Apparently a good method as they all arrived intact.


We drove out to Cape Forchu, just outside Yarmouth, to see the light station with its rare apple core design.  It is situated on a dramatic coastline with unusual rock formations.  We went later in the day so that we could see the “Cat”, the catamaran that travels between Yarmouth and Bar Harbor, ME. Yarmouth is also a very historic city, founded in 1651 and we spent time wandering the downtown as well as the harbour.


  Leaving Yarmouth it was very foggy and we took a chance that we would have a view when we went to Cape Sable Island.  We stood on the point of the mainland that is furthest south in the Maritimes. (There are islands further out with a lighthouse).  We were fortunate that the fog cleared while we were there and we were able to see the lighthouse which at 101 ft. is the tallest in Nova Scotia. 


We continued following the coast and headed northeast along the Atlantic where the waves are much bigger and the rocks of the coastline are very rugged.




There are rocky coves and bays but there are also wonderful sand beaches such as Carter’s beach.  The sand here is powder-fine just like the Caribbean.


The local road winds and twists close to the water.  After our experience with Hwy 215 we decided to keep to the limited access highway.  We did our touring with the truck only, which makes it easier to get to out of the way places.


Our next bases will be the Mahone Bay/Halifax area and I’ll write from there.


Bernie & Ross
